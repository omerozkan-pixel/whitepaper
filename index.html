Section II — The Solution
The Hybrid Membership Model 🐎
The Hybrid Model complements traditional chapter-based membership with direct individual memberships and institutional partnerships, aligning Servas governance with the Swiss Civil Code and international best practices for iNGOs. It is a necessary precondition for the Peace Builders Program.
🌳
SI Chapter
Legal Person · SW Motion 1, 5, 6
Registered Servas groups that form the collective backbone of SI. Must be a legal entity under national jurisdiction. Full GA voting rights.
OAK 🌳 — existing backbone

Natural Person · SW Motion 2
Experienced individuals (5+ years Servas membership) who join SI directly. Right to speak, vote, and submit motions at SIGA and Distant Votes.
OWL 🦉 — target: 55 in 5 years
🌹
SI Representative
Natural Person · SW Motion 7
A natural person appointed by groups ineligible for Chapter status (political/legal constraints). Brings structurally invisible regions into governance.
ROSE 🌹 — target: 25 in 5 years
🌻
SI Social Group
Legal Person · SW Motion 3
Registered NGOs/associations aligned with Servas' peace mission. One delegate with full GA voting rights. 300 CHF/year or donation-based.
SUNFLOWER 🌻 — target: 55 in 5 years
Critical clarification on Social Groups: When an NGO joins as an SI Social Group Member, this grants rights only to the organisation as a legal entity — specifically the right to appoint ONE delegate. Individual employees or volunteers of that organisation do NOT automatically become Servas members. Institutional and individual membership are fully separate.

The Multiplier Effect — Home-Schooling

How it works: Each certified PBE can award up to 5 credits to Seekers (hosts or travelers) during stays or Servas meetings. A 30-minute structured peace conversation replaces a passive overnight visit. ServasOnline marks PBEs and tracks credits. The Peace Builders Committee oversees quality.

Module 5
Conflict Transformation & Mediation Basics · 12 hrs
★ Primary AVP integration module — Transforming Power in action
▾

This module draws inspiration from Dostoyevsky's characters — figures who live the tensions of freedom, conscience, and responsibility — offering unparalleled metaphors for conflict dynamics. The Grand Inquisitor's argument for restricting freedom "for the sake of the many" provides a powerful pedagogical bridge to power dynamics in conflict intervention.

Content: Types and dynamics of conflict · Non-violent conflict transformation · Community-based resolution and mediation basics · Digital conflict scenario: resolving disinformation → polarisation on social media

Practice: Conflict mapping and simulations · Mediation role-play in an online disinformation case · AVP Hassle Lines exercise in pairs and triads · AVP extended Role Play: full scenario with debrief and alternative-path exploration
🌿 AVP — PRIMARY MODULE: Hassle Lines (two lines act out conflict, then find non-violent resolution — makes Transforming Power viscerally real). Role Play from multiple perspectives. Capstone: participants recall a real conflict and map it onto the AVP model — identifying where Transforming Power could have changed the outcome. This is the emotionally most intense module.

AVP Integration — Full Overview

Its integration does not introduce religious doctrine — it introduces an evidence-based conflict transformation methodology. AVP graduates may additionally earn dual certification: Servas Peace Builder + AVP Basic Workshop status.

Appendix C — 18 Motions
Voting Guide for Delegates
Each motion below states in plain language what passage means in practice, and what remaining on the status quo means. Click any motion to expand. Motions are grouped as in Appendix C of the Whitepaper.
I — Renaming & Core Structure (SW Motion 1, 17)
SW Motion 1
Rename Servas Organs
Standardise terminology for a professional iNGO structure
▾
✓ If passed
All official documents adopt: "SI Chapter" (was Servas Group), "Spokesperson" (was National Secretary), "SI Group" (was National Servas Group), "Seekers" replacing Hosts/Travelers. Servas aligns with Swiss Civil Code and iNGO norms.
✗ If not passed
Legacy terminology remains. Inconsistencies with the Swiss Civil Code framework continue. Motions 2, 3, 4, 7, 8 can still proceed but with unclear reference terms — creating governance ambiguity.
Note: This is a prerequisite motion. Rejecting it doesn't block the Hybrid Model but reduces structural clarity across all other motions.
SW Motion 17
Seekers Identity
Redefine all members as active "Seekers" rather than passive Hosts or Travelers
▾
✓ If passed
The identity of every Servas participant shifts from a transactional role (hosting, traveling) to a mission-driven identity. "Seeker" appears on membership cards, ServasOnline profiles, and all official communications. This directly addresses Bob Luitweiler's "cheap housing" critique.
✗ If not passed
Host/Traveler distinction remains. The philosophical reorientation — from passive observer to active peace agent — is not formalised in the Statutes.
II — Individual & Social Group Membership (SW Motions 2, 3, 7, 8)
SW Motion 2
SI Individual Member — Owl 🦉
Allow experienced individuals (5+ years) to join SI directly with full voting rights
▾
✓ If passed
Experienced members (5+ years, fee-paying) may apply directly to SI: speak, vote, and submit motions at SIGA and Distant Votes independently of their Chapter. Individual membership coexists with Chapter membership — it does not replace it. Target: 55 Owls in 5 years.
✗ If not passed
Individual direct membership remains impossible. Governance continues through chapter delegates only. The democratic deficit identified in the 2024 Audit is not addressed structurally. Motions 13 and 14 lose their primary beneficiaries.
Key condition: 5+ years Servas membership. Individual and Chapter membership fully coexist.

SW Motion 3
SI Social Group — Sunflower 🌻
Allow registered NGOs aligned with Servas' mission to become institutional members
▾
✓ If passed
Registered NGOs in peace, human rights, environmental fields may apply. Each appoints ONE delegate with GA voting rights. Pilot model: Year 1 free (observer), Years 2–3 at 60 CHF (partner), Year 4+ at 300 CHF (full member). Target: 55 Sunflowers in 5 years.
✗ If not passed
Civil society organisations cannot formally join SI. Youth pipeline, SDG alignment, and grant eligibility advantages are inaccessible. Revenue diversification limited to chapter fees and donations — the structural vulnerability the Audit Committee warned against.

SW Motion 7
SI Representative — Rose 🌹
Give groups ineligible for Chapter status a formal individual representative at SI
▾
✓ If passed
Groups in politically or legally constrained regions (Algeria, Nigeria, Zimbabwe, etc.) can nominate a natural person as SI Representative with full GA voting and motion rights. Approved by SIGA or SI Exco. Target: 25 Roses in 5 years.
✗ If not passed
Countries unable to register as formal chapters remain structurally invisible in SI governance. Their members have no vote and no formal voice at SIGA regardless of commitment or activity level.
SW Motion 8
Formalise Four Membership Types
Write the four new member categories into the SI Statutes
▾
✓ If passed
Statutes formally recognise: SI Chapter (Legal Person), SI Social Group (Legal Person), SI Individual (Natural Person), SI Representative (Natural Person). Aligns Servas with the Swiss Civil Code and creates the legal foundation for all other Hybrid Model motions.
✗ If not passed
Even if Motions 2, 3, and 7 pass individually, the Statutes remain internally inconsistent. SI lacks a clear legal framework — creating governance uncertainty for new member types.
Note: This is the legal consolidation motion. It codifies what Motions 2, 3, and 7 create.
III — Chapter Obligations & SI Exco Authority (SW Motions 4, 5, 6)
SW Motion 4
Exco Approval for Memberships
Let SI Exco approve new applications — eliminating 2–3 year SIGA wait
▾
✓ If passed
SI Exco gains authority alongside SIGA to approve memberships for groups with 10+ hosts. New groups can join in months, not years — critical for meeting 5-year KPI targets.
✗ If not passed
New chapters and social groups still require full SIGA approval. Pilot growth targets are harder to achieve within the 5-year KPI window.
SW Motion 5
Admission / Loss of Chapter Status
Define clear criteria for when a Chapter gains or loses its status
▾
✓ If passed
Status formally lost if: no spokesperson for 2 years, fewer than 10 hosts, or failure to meet obligations. Prevents dormant chapters from distorting the democratic count.
✗ If not passed
No formal mechanism to address inactive chapters. GA voting pool can include chapters that no longer function, weakening democratic legitimacy.
SW Motion 6
Legal Person Requirement for Chapters
Chapters must be registered legal entities under their national jurisdiction
▾
✓ If passed
Formal alignment with the Swiss Civil Code. Unregistered groups are reclassified as SI Groups — preserving their participation but removing GA voting rights until registration is achieved.
✗ If not passed
Swiss Civil Code distinction cannot be enforced in Servas governance. The Hybrid Model's legal architecture is weakened at its foundation.
Note: Groups in countries where registration is difficult should transition to SI Representative status (Motion 7), not be excluded.

▾
✓ If passed
Individual Members and SI Representatives have the same speaking and voting rights as Chapter delegates at SIGA. This is the central democratic opening of the Hybrid Model — without it, new member types exist on paper but are mute in practice.

Even if Motions 2, 3, 7 pass, new members have no formal voice at the GA. Democratic reform is structurally incomplete.

Distant Voting Rights for All Member Types
Extend inter-SIGA voting to Individual and Social Group members
▾

16,000+ Seekers who are not formal delegates remain voiceless in SI governance. Democratic renewal is limited to the smaller group of admitted Individual Members.

⏰Why change is needed — and why now
Q.1
Why do we need a hybrid membership model now?
Structure
Explains the convergence of three crises: demographic imbalance (60% over 60), financial fragility (CHF 12,331 deficit in 2024), and structural stagnation (sub-1% growth for 30 years). Argues 2026 is the last comfortable window for reform. See Section I, Cost of Inaction table.
Q.11
Is this primarily a financial reform?
Finance
No. Financial resilience is a necessary condition, not the goal. The goal is a "conscious community of free, self-determining spirits." Finance is the foundation, not the vision.
Q.14
"Isn't this really about money rather than values?"
Values
Addresses the concern directly. Structural reform is the vehicle; peace mission renewal is the destination. The Whitepaper is framed as a values-based evolution — not a financial rescue plan.
Q.20
"How does this proposal remain faithful to the spirit of Servas?"
Values
Connects the proposal to Bob Luitweiler's original Work-Study-Travel vision and his explicit critique of Servas becoming a "cheap housing program." The Hybrid Model is framed as restoration, not revolution.
Q.21
"Is this proposal philosophical rather than practical?"
Values
The philosophical and operational dimensions are intentionally integrated. Main sections stand independently for practitioner readers. Appendix F (Visionary Foundations: Kant, Dostoyevsky, Steiner) provides full philosophical depth for those who want it.
Q.22
Who gave the mandate to write this Whitepaper? 🇩🇰
Mandate
No external mandate was given. The document stems from 25 years of Servas membership and individual moral responsibility — the same impulse that led Bob Luitweiler to cycle across Denmark in 1949 without waiting for permission. See Appendix F for the philosophical grounding of this approach.
Q.27
Is the mention of Kant, Dostoyevsky, and Engels necessary? 🇩🇰
Values
Main sections streamlined so philosophical references don't interrupt the operational argument. Full framework is in Appendix F. The references are retained because this is a values-based evolution, not merely a technical reform — they provide the ethical justification for why individuals, not just institutions, should have a voice.
🗳️Democracy, governance & power
Q.3
How does individual membership improve democracy?
Democracy
Currently all decision-making passes through a small number of Chapter delegates. Individual membership gives experienced members (5+ years) direct speaking, voting, and motion-submission rights — breaking the bottleneck of National Secretary gatekeeping. See Motion 2, 13.
Q.4
Will National Secretaries lose their role or authority?
Democracy
No. National Secretaries become "Spokespeople" with a broader coordination role. The reform reduces their monopoly on agenda-setting and motion submission (Motion 15), but their leadership function within chapters remains fully intact.
Q.8
Is there a risk that individual members will dominate decision-making?
Democracy
Unlikely in the short term given the 5-year eligibility requirement and target cap (55 Owls vs. 100+ chapters). Long-term balance is managed through the voting rights framework in Motions 9, 13, and 14 — one entity, one vote principle.
Q.15
"Isn't direct or broader democracy risky and inefficient?"
Democracy
Consultative voting (non-binding) and individual member voting (binding) are distinct. The former guides; the latter decides. The GA structure prevents any single group from dominating through the one-entity-one-vote principle applied to Legal Persons.
Q.16
"Will large countries or individuals dominate smaller chapters?"
Democracy
Each Chapter retains one vote regardless of size. Individual Members add voices but do not multiply votes per country. The model is designed to balance, not amplify, geographic power imbalances.
Q.29
Will adding more member types really strengthen democracy in SI? 🇩🇰
Democracy
Yes — through four concrete mechanisms: (1) Direct individual representation for Owls (Motion 2, 13). (2) Inclusion of invisible regions via Roses (Motion 7). (3) Member-led motions (Motion 15). (4) Consultative voting for all 16,000+ Seekers (Motion 16). The GA becomes not just larger but qualitatively more representative.
💰Finance & sustainability
Q.7
How does the Hybrid Model affect Servas finances?
Finance
Projects +55,555 CHF additional annual income from individual and social group memberships (KPI #3). Diversifies away from sole chapter-fee dependency — which the 2024 Audit Committee explicitly identified as a structural vulnerability that must be resolved.
Q.30
Response to Mei Wang (SI Exco Co-Peace Secretary): complexity, fees, paid staff, trainer readiness
Expert ReviewFinance
Four-part response: (1) Complexity is real but unavoidable — raising chapter fees alone doesn't address structural stagnation and risks losing Global South chapters. (2) Doubling fees generates ~50-60K CHF but hits a structural ceiling — the membership base hasn't grown in 30 years. (3) Paid staff is her strongest point — the Hybrid Model's new revenue streams are designed to fund a paid Peace Builders Coordinator by Year 3. (4) Peace Schools and Peace Builders run in parallel; the program creates its own trainer pipeline from existing Peace School facilitators and AVP-certified trainers.
🌻Social Groups (NGOs & civil society)
Q.6
What are SI Social Group Members, and why include them?
Social Groups
NGOs and civil society organisations aligned with Servas' peace mission. They bring fresh perspectives, a youth pipeline, and grant eligibility advantages. Each sends ONE delegate with full voting rights. The value proposition for Social Groups is detailed in Section II — a two-way relationship stated without embellishment.
Q.23
Why should larger organisations be interested in SI Social Group Membership? 🇩🇰
Social Groups
Six concrete benefits: (1) Access to 13,125+ hosts as pre-vetted volunteers — no recruitment cost. (2) UN consultative status channel for peace diplomacy. (3) Full GA governance voice — formal co-ownership of a 70-year iNGO. (4) Peace Builders curriculum co-design rights. (5) Institutional credibility for grant applications. (6) SDG alignment framework (SDG 5, 13, 16, 17) for impact reporting.
Q.24
Will all members of a Social Group automatically become Servas members? 🇩🇰
Social Groups
No. Institutional and individual membership are fully separate. When an NGO joins as a Social Group Member, this grants rights only to the organisation as a legal entity — specifically the right to appoint ONE delegate. Employees, volunteers, and individual members of that organisation do NOT become Servas members. They must apply individually through a Servas Branch. This is grounded in the Swiss Civil Code distinction between Natural and Legal Persons.
Q.26
Can collective members water down the goals and vision of Servas? 🇩🇰
Social Groups
Addressed through four structural safeguards: (1) Strict mission alignment — must work in peace, human rights, or environmental fields. (2) Single delegate rule — one vote regardless of organisation size. (3) SIGA/Exco vetting process for every application. (4) Annual review mechanism to adjust or pause admissions. The Whitepaper frames inclusion as adding new channels to a pond that is currently drying up — not diluting it.
Q.28
Should Servas be careful about admitting organisations with religious backgrounds (e.g. AVP)? 🇩🇰
Social Groups
Yes — and the Whitepaper is explicit: mission alignment, not doctrinal origin, is the criterion. AVP has Quaker historical roots but is explicitly non-denominational and secular. Its conflict transformation methodology (Transforming Power, Good Within Everyone) is validated through secular research in prisons, schools, and businesses. Servas should welcome people and organisations that support the Servas vision and mission, regardless of their origin.
🕊️Peace Builders Program
Q.9
What is the Peace Builders program, and why is it central?
Peace
A 100-hour hybrid curriculum (6–8 weeks) producing certified Peace Builder Educators (PBEs). Paris pilot in 2027, expanding to USA, India, Argentina, Kenya by 2033. Graduates train others during host/traveler stays — the multiplier model. It is the operational expression of Servas' peace mission and the reason the Hybrid Model's financial resilience matters.
Q.19
"Is the Peace Builders project too ambitious or idealistic?"
Peace
Addressed with budget figures (36,000 CHF/3 years), Rotary's Rotaract precedent (1.4M members after similar reform), and the home-schooling multiplier model that allows scaling without large central budgets. Peace Schools and Peace Builders run in parallel to build the trainer pipeline progressively.
Q.25
What is being done about the critically low number of young members? 🇩🇰
Peace
Five-layer strategy: (1) University Campus Project — ambassadors, Peace Clubs, SYLE program. (2) Academic/career incentives — university credits, UN certificates, Cultural Competency Portfolios as CVs. (3) Digital identity overhaul — TikTok/Reels storytelling, gamification with digital badges. (4) Peace Builders Program — 100-hour curriculum with UNESCO accreditation target. (5) Participatory governance — direct voting for Individual Members, consultative voting for all Seekers. Target: 18–40 cohort from 15% to 20%.
🌳Chapters, structure & transition
Q.2
Does this model replace SI Chapters?
Structure
No. Chapters remain the backbone of Servas. The Hybrid Model adds new paths alongside chapters — it does not remove, replace, or diminish the Oak structure. Chapters retain full voting rights and their central role in the community.
Q.5
How are groups without legal status affected?
Structure
Groups that cannot register as Legal Persons (political or legal constraints) are not excluded — they transition to SI Group status and can appoint an SI Representative (Rose, Motion 7) with full GA voting and motion rights. No group is left voiceless under the Hybrid Model.
Q.10
How will this transition be managed?
Structure
Phased implementation with annual review points. Early years focus on learning, not rapid growth. The Innovation Project Team (appointed by SI Exco following Motion 18) leads implementation. 12 KPIs with named owners (IPT) track progress. It is a living roadmap, not a fixed prescription.
Q.12
What decision are delegates being asked to make?
Structure
Not to approve a finished system — to authorise a conscious process of renewal. Pass 18 motions that create the legal and structural framework, then entrust implementation to the Innovation Project Team with annual oversight by SI Exco. The roadmap can be adjusted, corrected, or paused at any annual review point.
Q.13
How will the Hybrid Model be reviewed?
Structure
Annual evaluation points built into the roadmap. SI Exco and the Innovation Project Team can adjust, correct, or pause elements that do not serve the community. The Risk-Mitigation Matrix (Section IV) identifies 10 risks with specific response strategies. See Section V for KPIs and owners.
Q.17
"Isn't the model too complex to implement?"
Structure
Complexity is acknowledged honestly. Managed through phased rollout, annual reviews, clear KPIs with named owners, and the Innovation Project Team. The alternative — structural status quo — carries its own compounding risk: decline that becomes progressively harder to reverse.
Q.18
"What if the Hybrid Model does not work as expected?"
Structure
The Risk-Mitigation Matrix (Section IV) addresses 10 identified risks with specific response strategies. Annual review authority allows adjustment. The model is explicitly designed to be corrected at regular intervals — not just launched and left to run.
