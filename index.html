Servas Whitepaper 2026 — 18 Motions
Voting Guide for Delegates
Each motion below states in plain language what passage means in practice, and what remaining on the status quo means. Click any motion to expand. Motions are grouped as they appear in the Motions Summary (Appendix C).
I — Renaming & Core Structure (SW Motion 1, 17)
SW Motion 1
Rename Servas Organs
Standardise terminology across all SI bodies for a professional iNGO identity
▾
If passed
All official documents and ServasOnline adopt new names: "SI Chapter" (formerly Servas Group), "Spokesperson" (formerly National Secretary), "SI Group" (formerly National Servas Group), "Seekers" replacing Hosts/Travelers.
If not passed
Legacy terminology remains. Inconsistencies between internal usage and the Swiss Civil Code framework continue. The Hybrid Model can still proceed but loses structural clarity.
Note: This is a prerequisite motion. Many other motions reference the new terminology. Rejecting this motion complicates the language of SW Motions 2, 3, 4, 7, 8.
SW Motion 17
Seekers Identity
Redefine all members as active "Seekers" rather than passive Hosts or Travelers
▾
If passed
The identity of every Servas participant shifts from a transactional role (hosting, traveling) to a mission-driven identity. "Seeker" appears on membership cards, ServasOnline profiles, and official communications.
If not passed
Host/Traveler distinction remains. The philosophical reorientation Bob Luitweiler called for — away from "cheap housing" toward active peace engagement — is not formalised.
II — Individual & Social Group Membership (SW Motions 2, 3, 7, 8)
SW Motion 2
SI Individual Member — Owl 🦉
Allow experienced individuals (5+ years) to join SI directly with full voting rights
▾
If passed
Experienced members (5+ years, fee-paying) may apply directly to SI for full membership: speak, vote, and submit motions at SIGA and Distant Votes independently of their Chapter. Target: 55 Owls in 5 years.
If not passed
Individual direct membership remains impossible. Governance continues to depend exclusively on chapter delegates. The democratic deficit identified in the Financial Audit is not addressed structurally.
Key condition: Applicant must be a Servas member for at least 5 years. Individual membership does not replace Chapter membership — both can coexist.
→ See Q&A #3, #8, #16 for democracy concerns
SW Motion 3
SI Social Group — Sunflower 🌻
Allow registered NGOs aligned with Servas' mission to become institutional members
▾
If passed
Registered NGOs in peace, human rights, and environmental fields may apply. Each appoints one delegate with GA voting rights. Annual fee: 300 CHF (or donation-based). Pilot observer phase available (Year 1 free, Year 2–3 at 60 CHF). Target: 55 Sunflowers in 5 years.
If not passed
Civil society organisations cannot formally join SI. The youth pipeline, SDG alignment, and grant eligibility advantages that Social Group partnerships create are not accessible. Revenue diversification is limited to chapter fees and donations only.
Critical clarification: Individual employees of Social Groups do NOT automatically become Servas members. Institutional and individual membership are fully separate.
→ See Q&A #6, #23, #24, #26 for Social Group concerns
SW Motion 7
SI Representative — Rose 🌹
Give groups ineligible for Chapter status a formal individual representative at SI
▾
If passed
Groups in politically or legally constrained regions (e.g. Algeria, Nigeria, Zimbabwe) can nominate a natural person as their SI Representative. That person holds GA voting and motion rights. Approved by SIGA or SI Exco. Target: 25 Roses in 5 years.
If not passed
Countries unable to register as formal chapters remain structurally invisible in SI governance. Their members have no vote and no voice at SIGA, regardless of commitment or activity.
→ See Q&A #5 for unregistered groups
SW Motion 8
Formalise Four Membership Types
Write the four new member categories into the SI Statutes
▾
If passed
The Statutes formally recognise: SI Chapter (Legal Person), SI Social Group (Legal Person), SI Individual (Natural Person), SI Representative (Natural Person). This aligns Servas with the Swiss Civil Code and creates the legal foundation for all other Hybrid Model motions.
If not passed
Even if SW Motions 2, 3, and 7 pass individually, the Statutes remain inconsistent. SI lacks a clear legal framework for the new membership types, creating governance uncertainty.
Note: This is the legal consolidation motion. It codifies what Motions 2, 3, and 7 create.
III — Chapter Obligations & SI Exco Authority (SW Motions 4, 5, 6)
SW Motion 4
Exco Approval for Memberships
Let SI Exco (not only SIGA) approve new Chapter applications to speed up admission
▾
If passed
SI Exco gains authority alongside SIGA to approve memberships for groups with 10+ hosts. This eliminates 2–3 year waits for approval at the next General Assembly. New groups can join in months, not years.
If not passed
New chapters and social groups still require full SIGA approval. The pilot growth targets are harder to reach within the 5-year KPI window.
SW Motion 5
Admission / Loss of Chapter Status
Define clear criteria for when a Chapter gains or loses its status
▾
If passed
Status is formally lost if: no spokesperson for 2 years, fewer than 10 hosts, or failure to meet obligations. This protects governance quality and prevents dormant chapters from distorting the democratic count.
If not passed
No formal mechanism exists to address inactive or non-compliant chapters. The GA voting pool can include chapters that no longer function, weakening democratic legitimacy.
SW Motion 6
Legal Person Requirement for Chapters
Chapters must be registered legal entities under their national jurisdiction
▾
If passed
SI aligns formally with the Swiss Civil Code. Chapters that are not registered legal persons are reclassified as SI Groups (not Chapters), preserving their participation but removing GA voting rights until registration is achieved.
If not passed
The Swiss Civil Code distinction between Natural and Legal Persons cannot be enforced in Servas governance. The Hybrid Model's legal architecture is weakened.
Note: This motion may affect chapters in countries where iNGO registration is difficult. SI Exco should prepare a support framework for affected groups.
→ See Q&A #5 for groups without legal status
IV — Governance, Voting & Participation (SW Motions 9–16)
SW Motion 9
GA Delegation Rules
Define who constitutes a delegation at SIGA under the Hybrid Model
▾
If passed
One delegate per Legal Person (Chapter / Social Group) and each Natural Person member (Individual / Representative) present. Clear, enforceable rules for hybrid assemblies.
If not passed
GA delegation rules remain undefined for the new member types. Individual and Social Group members have no clear procedural standing even if admitted via Motions 2, 3, 7.
SW Motion 10
Online General Assembly (O-SIGA)
Authorise SI Exco to call an Annual Online General Assembly
▾
If passed
An Annual Online GA (O-SIGA) can be held in the years between in-person SIGAs. Replaces or supplements Distant Voting with a real-time deliberative forum accessible to all member types globally.
If not passed
Governance decisions remain concentrated in the 3-year SIGA cycle. New individual and social group members have limited opportunities to exercise their rights between conferences.
SW Motion 11
Item Submission Rights
Expand who can submit agenda items to SIGA
▾
If passed
Legal Persons (Chapters, Social Groups), Individual Members, and SI Exco can all submit items. Combined with SW Motion 15, this opens agenda-setting to the full membership.
If not passed
Agenda control remains with National Secretaries and SI Exco. Individual members and Social Groups have no formal right to raise issues at the GA even if admitted.
SW Motion 12
Decisions of SIGA — Recording
Mandate distribution of GA decisions to all SI Members
▾
If passed
Decisions must be formally recorded and distributed to all SI Members — not just chapter spokespeople. Individual and Social Group members stay informed and accountable to the same standard.
If not passed
Transparency gap remains. New member types may participate in votes but receive no formal communication about outcomes. KPI #10 (transparency) is harder to achieve.
SW Motion 13
GA Voting Rights for All Member Types
Grant all SI Member types the right to speak and vote at SIGA
▾
If passed
Individual Members and SI Representatives have the same speaking and voting rights as Chapter delegates at SIGA. This is the central democratic opening of the Hybrid Model.
If not passed
Even if new member types are created (Motions 2, 3, 7), they have no formal voice at the GA. The democratic reform is incomplete — new members exist on paper but are mute in practice.
→ See Q&A #3, #8, #29 for democracy questions
SW Motion 14
Distant Voting Rights for All Member Types
Extend inter-SIGA voting to Individual and Social Group members
▾
If passed
All SI Member types participate in Distant Votes between SIGAs. Individual members living far from chapter headquarters are no longer excluded from inter-cycle governance.
If not passed
Distant Voting remains a chapter-only mechanism. Individual and Social Group members vote at SIGA but not between SIGAs — a two-tier participation structure.
SW Motion 15
Member-Led Motions
Allow five chapter members jointly to submit a motion — bypassing sole NS approval
▾
If passed
Any five Seekers from an SI Chapter can jointly submit a motion to SIGA. A National Secretary can no longer unilaterally block members from raising issues. This is a significant anti-bottleneck reform.
If not passed
Motion submission remains in the hands of National Secretaries and SI Exco. Members who disagree with their NS have no formal mechanism to raise concerns at the GA.
SW Motion 16
Consultative Voting
Introduce online non-binding pre-votes open to all Seekers before formal GA decisions
▾
If passed
Before any formal SIGA or Distant Vote, all Seekers (hosts and travelers) can cast a non-binding consultative vote. Results are shared with delegates before they vote. Broader membership voice, richer decisions. KPI #11: 5 consultative votes in 5 years.
If not passed
The 16,000+ Seekers who are not formal delegates remain voiceless in SI governance. Democratic renewal is limited to the smaller group of admitted Individual Members.
→ See Q&A #15, #29 for democracy risk concerns
V — Peace Builders Project (SW Motion 18)
SW Motion 18
Peace Builders Project
Create a professional team and fund the 100-hour Peace Builders Education Program
▾
If passed
A dedicated Peace Builders Committee (3–5 people) is established. SI commits 60,000 CHF over 3 years (from donations and Hybrid Model revenue). Paris pilot launches 2027. Graduates become certified Peace Builder Educators (PBEs) who train others during host/traveler stays. KPI #4: 5 seminars on 5 continents in 5 years.
If not passed
The Hybrid Model's "visionary pillar" does not launch. Servas remains a hosting network without a structured peace education offering. The demographic renewal and youth engagement strategy loses its primary program vehicle.
On trainer readiness: The first PBE cohort is drawn from existing Peace School facilitators and AVP-certified trainers. Peace Schools and Peace Builders develop in parallel — not in sequence. The program creates its own trainer pipeline.
→ See Q&A #9, #19, #30 for program concerns
Appendix B — 30 Questions & Answers
Q&A Index by Topic
The Q&A section (Appendix B) contains 30 questions. Click any question to see a brief summary. Questions marked 🇩🇰 were raised at the Servas Denmark General Assembly (April 2026).
How to use this index: Find your concern in the topic that matters most to you, then go directly to that Q&A number in the Whitepaper. If you are new to the Whitepaper, start with Q.1, Q.7, Q.9, and Q.12.
⏰ Why change is needed — and why now
Q.1
Why do we need a hybrid membership model now?
Context
Explains the convergence of three crises: demographic imbalance (60% over 60), financial fragility (CHF 12,331 deficit in 2024), and structural stagnation (sub-1% growth for 30 years). Argues 2026 is the last comfortable window for reform.
Q.11
Is this primarily a financial reform?
Finance
No. Financial resilience is a necessary condition, not the goal. The goal is a "conscious community of free, self-determining spirits." Finance is the foundation, not the vision.
Q.14
"Isn't this really about money rather than values?"
Values
Addresses the concern directly: the Hybrid Model is framed as a values-based evolution. Structural reform is the vehicle; peace mission renewal is the destination.
Q.20
"How does this proposal remain faithful to the spirit of Servas?"
Values
Connects the proposal back to Bob Luitweiler's original Work-Study-Travel vision and his explicit critique of Servas becoming a "cheap housing program." The Hybrid Model is framed as restoration, not revolution.
Q.22
Who gave the mandate to write this Whitepaper? 🇩🇰
Mandate
No external mandate was given. The document stems from 25 years of Servas membership and individual moral responsibility — the same impulse that led Bob Luitweiler to cycle across Denmark in 1949 without waiting for permission.
🗳️ Democracy, governance & power
Q.3
How does individual membership improve democracy?
Democracy
Currently all decision-making passes through a small number of Chapter delegates. Individual membership gives experienced members direct speaking, voting, and motion-submission rights — bypassing the bottleneck of National Secretary gatekeeping.
Q.4
Will National Secretaries lose their role or authority?
Democracy
No. National Secretaries become "Spokespeople" with a broader coordination role. The reform reduces their monopoly on agenda-setting and motion submission, but their leadership function in chapters remains.
Q.8
Is there a risk that individual members will dominate decision-making?
Democracy
Unlikely in the short term given the 5-year eligibility requirement and the cap on target numbers (55 Owls vs. 100+ chapters). Over time, the balance is managed through the voting rights framework in Motions 9, 13, and 14.
Q.15
"Isn't direct or broader democracy risky and inefficient?"
Democracy
Consultative voting (non-binding) and individual member voting are distinct. The former guides; the latter decides. The GA structure prevents any single group from dominating through the one-entity-one-vote principle.
Q.16
"Will large countries or individuals dominate smaller chapters?"
Democracy
Each Chapter retains one vote regardless of size. Individual Members add voices but do not multiply votes per country. The model is designed to balance, not amplify, geographic power.
Q.29
Will adding more member types really strengthen democracy in SI? 🇩🇰
Democracy
Yes — through four mechanisms: direct individual representation (Owls), inclusion of invisible regions (Roses), member-led motions (Motion 15), and consultative voting for all Seekers (Motion 16). The GA becomes not just larger but qualitatively more representative.
💰 Finance & sustainability
Q.7
How does the Hybrid Model affect Servas finances?
Finance
Projects +55,555 CHF additional annual income from individual and social group memberships. Diversifies away from sole chapter-fee dependency, which the 2024 Audit Committee explicitly identified as a structural vulnerability.
Q.30
Response to Mei Wang: complexity, fees, paid staff, trainer readiness
Expert review
Four-part response to the SI Exco Co-Peace Secretary's critique: (1) Complexity is real but unavoidable; raising chapter fees alone cannot substitute for structural diversification. (2) Fee doubling generates ~50-60K CHF but doesn't resolve 30-year stagnation. (3) Paid staff is the strongest point — addressed as a Year 3 milestone once revenue targets are met. (4) Peace Schools and Peace Builders run in parallel, not sequence; the program creates its own trainer pipeline.
🌻 Social Groups (NGOs & civil society)
Q.6
What are SI Social Group Members, and why include them?
Social Groups
NGOs and civil society organisations aligned with Servas' peace mission. They bring fresh perspectives, youth pipelines, and grant eligibility advantages. Each sends one delegate with full voting rights.
Q.23
Why should larger organisations be interested in becoming SI Social Group Members? 🇩🇰
Social Groups
Six concrete benefits: access to 13,125+ hosts as volunteers, UN consultative status channel, full GA governance voice, Peace Builders curriculum co-design, institutional credibility for grant applications, SDG alignment framework for impact reporting.
Q.24
Will all members of a Social Group automatically become Servas members? 🇩🇰
Social Groups
No. Institutional and individual membership are fully separate. When an NGO joins as a Social Group, only the organisation as a legal entity gains rights — not its employees or volunteers. Those must apply individually through a Servas Branch.
Q.26
Can collective members water down the goals and vision of Servas? 🇩🇰
Social Groups
Addressed through four structural safeguards: strict mission alignment criteria, single delegate rule (one vote regardless of organisation size), SIGA/Exco vetting process, and annual review mechanism to pause or adjust admissions.
Q.28
Should Servas be careful about admitting organisations with religious backgrounds (e.g. AVP)? 🇩🇰
Social Groups
Yes — and the Whitepaper is clear: mission alignment, not doctrinal origin, is the criterion. AVP has Quaker roots but is explicitly non-denominational. Its conflict transformation methodology is validated through secular research in prisons, schools, and businesses.
🕊️ Peace Builders Program
Q.9
What is the Peace Builders program, and why is it central?
Peace
A 100-hour hybrid curriculum (6-8 weeks) producing certified Peace Builder Educators (PBEs). Paris pilot in 2027. Graduates train others during host/traveler stays — a multiplier model. It is the operational expression of Servas' peace mission.
Q.19
"Is the Peace Builders project too ambitious or idealistic?"
Peace
Addresses the concern directly with budget figures (60,000 CHF / 3 years), Rotary's Rotaract precedent, and the home-schooling multiplier model that allows the program to scale without requiring large central budgets.
Q.25
What is being done about the critically low number of young members? 🇩🇰
Peace
Five-layer strategy: University Campus Project (ambassadors, Peace Clubs, SYLE), academic/career incentives (university credits, UN certificates, CV portfolios), digital identity overhaul (TikTok, gamification), Peace Builders Program, and participatory governance (direct voting). Target: 18-40 cohort from 15% to 20%.
🌳 Chapters, structure & transition
Q.2
Does this model replace SI Chapters?
Structure
No. Chapters remain the backbone of Servas. The Hybrid Model adds new paths alongside chapters — it does not remove, replace, or diminish the Oak structure.
Q.5
How are groups without legal status affected?
Structure
Groups that cannot register as Legal Persons (due to political or legal constraints) are not excluded — they transition to SI Group status and can appoint an SI Representative (Rose) with full GA voting rights.
Q.10
How will this transition be managed?
Structure
Phased implementation with annual review points. Early years focus on learning, not rapid growth. The Innovation Project Team (appointed by SI Exco) leads implementation. Clear KPIs with named owners (IPT) track progress.
Q.12
What decision are delegates being asked to make?
Structure
Not to approve a finished system — to authorise a conscious process of renewal. Delegates are asked to pass 18 motions that create the legal and structural framework, then entrust implementation to the Innovation Project Team with annual oversight.
Q.13
How will the Hybrid Model be reviewed?
Structure
Annual evaluation points are built into the roadmap. The model is explicitly described as a "living process," not a fixed prescription. SI Exco and the Innovation Project Team can adjust, correct, or pause elements that do not serve the community.
Q.17
"Isn't the model too complex to implement?"
Structure
Complexity is acknowledged honestly. It is managed through phased rollout, annual reviews, clear KPIs with owners, and the Innovation Project Team. The alternative — structural status quo — carries its own risk of organisational decline.
Q.18
"What if the Hybrid Model does not work as expected?"
Structure
The Risk-Mitigation Matrix (Section IV) addresses 10 identified risks with specific response strategies. Annual review authority allows adjustment. The model is designed to be corrected, not just executed.
